# PART I — BRAND FOUNDATION

## 1. Brand Essence

Invita — بانگێشت نامە — is, at its essence, **a handwritten invitation reimagined for a glowing screen.** The Kurdish name itself, *bangeshtnama* ("invitation letter/document"), roots the brand in something older and more intimate than an app: the tradition of a formally penned, personally delivered letter of invitation. Every pixel of Invita should honor that origin — this is a *نامە* (a letter, a document of significance), not a notification.

The essence in one line: **an invitation should feel like it was written by hand, sealed in wax, and somehow, impossibly, arrived glowing on your phone.**

## 2. Brand Values

- **Warmth over spectacle** — the brand impresses through feeling, never through excess.
- **Craftsmanship** — every detail (a shadow's softness, a letter's spacing) is treated as handiwork, not default settings.
- **Cultural rootedness** — Invita is proudly Kurdish at its core (its name, its language, its warmth), while its visual grammar speaks a universal, international language of luxury. It must never feel like a translated product; it must feel native and original in Sorani.
- **Restraint** — the discipline to leave something out is valued as highly as the skill to add something beautiful.
- **Trustworthiness** — for a document announcing life's most important moments, visual confidence and stability are non-negotiable brand values.

## 3. Brand Emotion

The single emotional target for every Invita touchpoint: **the held breath before a beautiful reveal.** Not excitement (too loud), not happiness (too generic) — *anticipation resolving into warmth.* This is the emotion of watching a candle being lit in a dim room, of a heavy envelope being turned over to find the seal, of golden hour light through leaves. The brand's mood board reference — a candid, sunlit garden wedding portrait, soft focus, natural greenery — is the north star for this emotion: **real joy, softly lit, never staged.**

Secondary emotional notes, present in supporting amounts: nostalgia (for tradition and handwritten letters), pride (for the host presenting something beautiful), belonging (for the guest, being personally chosen).

## 4. Brand Story

Before text messages, before printed cards, an invitation was a *نامە* — a letter, often hand-delivered, sometimes sealed, always considered. Invita exists to bring that weight and warmth back into a world of disposable digital notifications. Its Kurdish name is not a localization decision — it is the founding idea: this is a *بانگێشت نامە*, a proper letter of invitation, built for a generation that lives on a phone screen but still longs for the ceremony of being truly invited to something.

Every future template extends this same origin story into a new occasion: a wedding نامە, a graduation نامە, a housewarming نامە — each one a letter, each one worthy of being kept.

## 5. Brand Personality

If بانگێشت نامە were a person:

- **The elder who writes beautifully by hand** — someone whose invitations were always the most anticipated to receive, not because they were loud, but because they were clearly made with care.
- **Warm, unhurried, dignified.** Never rushed, never casual to the point of carelessness.
- **Bilingual in feeling** — fluent in international luxury visual language, yet speaking, at heart, in Kurdish warmth.
- **Quietly proud.** Confident enough in its own taste that it never needs to over-explain or over-decorate.

Voice in UI copy (Sorani): short, warm, dignified sentences — the register of a beautifully worded formal invitation, not a casual chat app. Never uses slang, never uses exclamation-heavy phrasing, never borrows English words where a proper Sorani word exists.

## 6. Visual Philosophy

Every visual decision answers one question first: **does this feel like it was made by hand, with care, for this specific person?** Digital luxury brands often fail by mistaking "high-tech" for "high-end." Invita's visual philosophy inverts this: the most premium-feeling surface in this system is not the most technologically dazzling one, but the one that feels most *considered* — a soft gold line placed with the precision of a letterpress, a shadow as soft as candlelight.

Visual hierarchy is achieved primarily through **restraint, scale, and light** — never through color saturation, never through competing decorative elements. At any given moment, the eye should have exactly one place it wants to rest.

## 7. Luxury Philosophy

Luxury, in this system, is defined by four qualities, in order of importance:

1. **Material believability.** Glass looks like glass, gold looks like gold, paper looks like paper — digitally rendered materials must reference real-world physical luxury materials (fine cardstock, wax, brushed gold, frosted glass), not generic "app UI" surfaces.
2. **Unhurried pacing.** Luxury never rushes to make its point. Motion, reveal timing, and information density all breathe.
3. **Precision.** Alignment, spacing, and proportion are exact — luxury brands are recognized by the discipline of their grids as much as by their color palettes.
4. **Scarcity of gesture.** A luxury brand uses its boldest signature move (a gold accent, a signature transition) rarely and consistently — never sprinkles it everywhere it could technically fit.

## 8. Minimalism Philosophy

Invita's minimalism draws equally from **Japanese Ma (間)** — the meaningful use of empty space/pause — and **Scandinavian functional simplicity** — the idea that the most honest design is one where every element has an obvious, singular function. The test for every screen: if you removed any single element, would the invitation lose meaning? If not, that element is decoration, and decoration is the enemy of this brand's specific luxury (which is rooted in sincerity, not opulence).

Minimalism here is not "empty" or "cold" — it is *edited*. A single, perfect gold line under a name is minimalism. A blank screen with no warmth at all is just emptiness, and is equally wrong.

## 9. Emotional Design Philosophy

Design decisions are always sequenced: **feeling first, function second, decoration never.** Before a screen is evaluated for clarity or usability, it is evaluated for whether it produces the target emotion from Section 3. A perfectly usable, perfectly "clean" screen that produces no feeling has failed this brand's core purpose, even if it passes every usability heuristic.

Practically: warmth is engineered through light temperature (warm gold/amber over cool blue-white), through pacing (slow reveals over instant pop-ins), and through material softness (blurred, diffused edges over hard geometric ones).

## 10. Storytelling Philosophy

Every invitation — regardless of occasion — is composed as a **three-act letter**:

- **Act I — The Seal.** The invitation arrives closed, composed, quiet. A moment of anticipation before anything is revealed (visually: a sealed envelope, a soft glow, a single line of Doran type fading in).
- **Act II — The Letter Opens.** Content is revealed progressively — names, the occasion, imagery, tone — paced like pages turning, never dumped as a single screen.
- **Act III — The Details, Kept Elegant.** Practical information (date, time, location, RSVP) arrives last, but is never allowed to look like "the boring part" — it is dressed in the same gold, glass, and Doran typography as the emotional opening.

This structure is universal across all future templates; only the *content* and *accent color* of each act change per occasion (see Section 30).

# PART II — VISUAL SYSTEMS

## 11. Color System

Invita's palette is built on a **warm, muted, candlelit luxury register** — directly informed by the golden-hour, botanical-garden mood of the brand's reference imagery, and by the material language of fine stationery: ivory cardstock, gold foil, dark ink, soft botanical green. No color in this system is ever used at full saturation; every hue is muted, as if lit by warm ambient light rather than a screen backlight.

### 11.1 Primary Colors
- **Invita Ivory** (near-white, warm-toned, e.g. the range of warm off-whites like #FBF7F0–#F7F1E8) — the primary canvas color. Used for: primary backgrounds in light contexts, card surfaces, the "paper" of the invitation. Emotional meaning: fine cardstock, cleanliness, calm.
- **Invita Ink** (deep warm charcoal-black, never pure black, e.g. #211D18–#2A2420) — the primary text and grounding color. Emotional meaning: the weight of handwritten ink; authority and legibility without harshness.

### 11.2 Secondary Colors
- **Botanical Sage** (muted, greyed-green, e.g. #7C8B6F–#8FA07E) — secondary accent for natural/organic occasions (Housewarming, Family Gathering, Festival). Emotional meaning: growth, home, nature, the garden mood of the brand reference photo.
- **Dusk Plum** (muted mauve-brown, e.g. #6B4E4A–#8A6560) — secondary accent for romantic/formal occasions (Wedding, Engagement). Emotional meaning: romance without cliché pink; dusk light.

### 11.3 Accent Colors
- **Warm Amber** (e.g. #C98A3E–#D9A05C) — the "living light" accent used for glows, highlights, and active states. Emotional meaning: candlelight, golden hour, warmth.
- Each future template is assigned exactly **one** occasion accent drawn from a shared, pre-approved accent family (Section 30 governs how new accents are approved) — never an arbitrary new hue per template.

### 11.4 Gold Material Colors
Gold in this system is a *material*, not a flat color, and must always be expressed as a gradient or lit surface, never a single flat gold hex:
- **Foil Gold** (bright, thin highlight edge, e.g. #E7C77D) — used only for the thinnest of edge highlights (a line under a name, a border edge catching light).
- **Antique Gold** (the gold "body" tone, e.g. #B8905A–#A87F4C) — used for icon strokes, dividers, seals, frame edges.
- **Shadow Gold** (the gold "recess" tone, e.g. #7A5A34) — used only in gradient transitions and never alone, to give gold dimensionality.
- **Rule:** any gold element must contain at least a two-stop gradient (Foil → Antique, or Antique → Shadow) to read as metallic rather than as a flat mustard/yellow, which reads cheap.

### 11.5 Background Colors
- **Primary Background:** Invita Ivory (light mode default — the platform's primary, signature mode, evoking daylight paper).
- **Deep Background:** Invita Ink-based near-black (e.g. #17140F) used for the Act I "sealed envelope" opening moment and any nighttime/formal-occasion templates (e.g., a Corporate Event or Award Ceremony evening template) — used deliberately as a *dramatic* alternate mode, not a default dark mode toggle.

### 11.6 Surface Colors
- **Elevated Surface** — Invita Ivory lifted 2–4% lighter with a barely-visible warm shadow, used for cards, panels, sheets.
- **Recessed Surface** — Ivory darkened 3–5% toward a warm grey, used for input fields and secondary containers, never a cool grey.

### 11.7 Glass Colors
- **Light Glass** — semi-transparent warm-white (approx. 12–20% opacity of Invita Ivory over content) for glass panels in light contexts.
- **Dark Glass** — semi-transparent warm-black (approx. 20–30% opacity of Invita Ink) for glass panels over imagery or in the Act I dark opening.
- Glass is always tinted warm — never a neutral or cool-grey glass, which would read as generic "frosted UI" rather than fine glassware.

### 11.8 Border Colors
- **Hairline Border** — Ink at 8–12% opacity over Ivory surfaces; Ivory at 10–15% opacity over dark surfaces. Used for card edges, input outlines.
- **Gold Border** — Antique Gold at reduced opacity (40–60%), reserved for a small number of "important frame" moments (photo frames, the RSVP card, a sealed envelope edge) — never used as a default border for ordinary UI elements.

### 11.9 Divider Colors
- **Standard Divider** — Ink at 6–8% opacity; nearly invisible, used only to create rhythm between sections, never as a strong graphic line.
- **Ceremonial Divider** — a thin Antique Gold rule, used exactly once per screen at most, to mark a genuinely significant transition (e.g., beneath a host's name, above the date).

### 11.10 Text Colors
- **Primary Text** — Invita Ink at full opacity, for names, headlines, and essential information.
- **Secondary Text** — Invita Ink at 65–75% opacity, for supporting sentences and descriptions.
- **Tertiary / Caption Text** — Invita Ink at 45–55% opacity, for captions, timestamps, footer text.
- **Inverse Text** — Invita Ivory at full/reduced opacity, for text over the Deep Background or over imagery.

### 11.11 Muted Colors
A single muted neutral family (warm greys derived from Ink, e.g. #8A8378 range) used for disabled states, placeholder text, and inactive icons — always warm-toned, never a cool grey pulled from a generic UI kit.

### 11.12 Semantic Colors (Success / Warning / Danger / Info)
Even semantic/system colors must be desaturated to fit the palette — they should look like they belong to the same fine-stationery world, not like a generic app's status colors:
- **Success** — muted sage-green (a deeper relative of Botanical Sage, e.g. #6B8F5E) — used sparingly, e.g. confirming an RSVP was sent.
- **Warning** — muted ochre/amber-brown (e.g. #B8823D), close to the gold family so it never feels alarming.
- **Danger** — muted brick/terracotta red (e.g. #A85C4A) — deliberately not a bright fire-engine red, to avoid breaking the palette's warmth even in an error state.
- **Info** — muted dusty blue (e.g. #6E7F8C) — the one intentionally cool color in the system, used *only* for neutral informational states (e.g., "link copied"), never for anything emotionally significant.
- **Rule:** semantic colors appear only in small, functional touches (an icon, a thin underline, a small badge) — never as a large fill or background, which would visually compete with the brand's core palette.

### 11.13 Overlay Colors
- **Scrim Overlay** — Invita Ink at 40–60% opacity, used under text placed over host-uploaded photography, to guarantee legibility without resorting to a hard box.
- **Reveal Overlay** — a soft radial gradient from transparent to Deep Background, used during Act I → Act II transitions to simulate a curtain lifting or an envelope opening.

### 11.14 Shadow Colors
All shadows are tinted, never neutral/cool black (see Section 16 for full shadow system):
- **Ambient Shadow** — Ink at very low opacity (4–8%), heavily blurred.
- **Gold Glow Shadow** — Warm Amber at low opacity (10–20%), used only around focal, "sacred" elements (a seal, a primary button, a countdown) to simulate candlelit glow.

### 11.15 Gradient System
- **Sunset Gradient** — Warm Amber → Dusk Plum, used for select Act I backgrounds and hero moments across romantic occasion templates.
- **Garden Gradient** — Botanical Sage → Invita Ivory, used for Housewarming and nature-themed occasions.
- **Gold Foil Gradient** — Foil Gold → Antique Gold → Shadow Gold (three-stop, see 11.4), used exclusively for metallic elements, never as a full-screen background.
- **Rule:** gradients are always soft, low-contrast between stops, and directional (typically top-to-bottom or radial from a light source) to simulate natural light falloff — never a sharp two-tone "app icon" gradient.

## 12. Typography System

### Why Doran Fits the Brand
Doran is Invita's **only** typeface, across every weight, every language context, every screen — used for both the brand's most emotional display moments and its most functional UI text, in Sorani Kurdish. This single-family discipline is itself a luxury signal: fine European fashion houses and stationers are recognized by using one typeface with total mastery, rather than blending several. Doran's letterforms — with the warmth and formality appropriate to a *بانگێشت نامە* — give Invita a typographic identity that is unmistakably its own from the first glance at a name on an envelope to the smallest caption in a footer. Because Doran is confirmed to fully and natively support Sorani script, the entire type system below is built on exactly two weights: **Doran Bold** and **Doran Medium** — no third weight, no substitute, no fallback face is introduced anywhere in the system.

### 12.1 The Scale

| Role | Weight | Relative Size | Usage | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Display** | Doran Bold | Largest in system | The host/couple/celebrant's name(s); the single most important word(s) on the invitation | Always given the most surrounding negative space in the entire layout; appears once or twice per invitation, never repeated as a UI pattern |
| **Hero** | Doran Bold | Very large | The occasion title (e.g., an occasion's title text) in Act I/II | Slightly tighter letter-spacing than Display to read as a strong statement rather than a name |
| **Section Title** | Doran Bold | Large | Titles introducing a new narrative beat (Act II sub-sections, Act III "details") | Consistent size across all templates — this is a structural, not decorative, size |
| **Card Title** | Doran Bold | Medium-large | Titles within a card/panel (e.g., a single detail card's heading) | Slightly smaller and less ornamental than Section Title to preserve hierarchy |
| **Body Large** | Doran Medium | Medium | Primary narrative sentences (Act II storytelling copy) | Generous line-height (see 12.2) since this carries the emotional prose |
| **Body** | Doran Medium | Base | Standard UI copy, descriptions, form labels' associated help text | The system's true "default" size; most on-screen Sorani text uses this |
| **Body Small** | Doran Medium | Base minus one step | Secondary descriptive text, list items | |
| **Caption** | Doran Medium | Small | Timestamps, photo credits, small print | Never below the accessible minimum size (see Section 26) |
| **Button** | Doran Bold | Base | All interactive call-to-action labels | Bold is used here deliberately — buttons are the one UI-chrome element allowed a confident weight, since they represent a moment of guest action |
| **Label** | Doran Medium | Small | Form field labels, tag/category labels | Slightly increased letter-spacing for clarity at small size |
| **Input** | Doran Medium | Base | Text entered/displayed inside form fields (RSVP name entry, guest count) | Matches Body size exactly, so typed text feels continuous with the rest of the experience, not like a separate "system font" input |
| **Countdown Numbers** | Doran Bold | Large, tabular | Countdown-to-event numerals | Numerals are treated with fixed (tabular) spacing so digits don't shift the layout as they change; see 12.3 for numeral direction |
| **Invitation Names** | Doran Bold | Same tier as Display | Guest's own name when a personalized link is used | Always rendered with the same weight and care as the host's own name — a guest should never feel like their name was an afterthought field |
| **Dates** | Doran Medium | Card Title tier | Event date | Given its own generous spacing line even when adjacent to time/location, since the date is often the single most-referenced fact in the invitation |
| **Location** | Doran Medium | Card Title tier | Venue name and address | Paired consistently with a location icon (Section 19) |
| **Footer Text** | Doran Medium | Smallest in system | Platform credit, legal/minimal text | Lowest visual priority by design; must still meet accessibility minimums |

### 12.2 Spacing, Weight, Line Height, Readability

- **Only two weights exist system-wide: Bold (emotional/structural emphasis) and Medium (everything else).** No Light, no Black, no italic variant is introduced — Doran's two weights must do all the work, which is precisely why the scale above leans on *size and spacing*, not additional weights, to build hierarchy.
- **Line height** scales inversely with text size: Display/Hero text uses tight, confident line-height (near 1.05–1.15× the type size) since large display text needs little help staying legible; Body and Body Small use generous line-height (1.5–1.65×) to keep dense Sorani paragraphs airy and unhurried, consistent with the brand's unhurried pacing principle (Section 7).
- **Letter-spacing** is slightly opened on Display/Hero sizes to give large type room to breathe (consistent with a fine-engraved-invitation feel), and kept at a natural, unmodified spacing at Body/Caption sizes to preserve Sorani script legibility — Arabic-script letterforms are connected and contextual, so artificially widened tracking at small sizes must be avoided as it can visually break letter-joining and harm readability.
- **Weight pairing rule:** never place two Bold elements immediately adjacent without a size or spacing break between them (e.g., a Bold Section Title directly above a Bold Card Title with no gap) — this collapses hierarchy. At least one of size, weight, or spacing must differ between any two adjacent text blocks.

### 12.3 Arabic-Script (Sorani) Layout Considerations
- **Direction:** All UI text flows right-to-left (RTL), matching Sorani's native Arabic-script direction. Layouts, icon placement (e.g., a "back" chevron), and reading order for narrative sequences must mirror accordingly — the *reading* order of Act I → II → III remains top-to-bottom, but all horizontal alignment, icon-to-text ordering, and list bullets follow RTL convention.
- **Numerals:** Countdown numbers, dates, and RSVP counts use clean tabular Doran numerals set left-to-right within an otherwise RTL context (the standard convention for numerals embedded in Arabic-script text), ensuring dates and countdowns remain instantly legible.
- **Diacritics and letter-joining:** Because Sorani script connects letterforms contextually, text scaling must always scale as whole text blocks (never letter-spaced apart, per 12.2) to preserve correct letter-joining at every size in the scale.
- **Text alignment default:** right-aligned for all body and label text; display/hero moments may use centered alignment deliberately for ceremonial emphasis (e.g., a centered name on an opening screen), but centered alignment is a considered exception, not the default.

## 13. Spacing System

Spacing in Invita is never arbitrary — it is built on a single base unit that scales geometrically, so that every margin, gap, and padding value in the product can be traced back to one number, reinforcing the "precision" luxury quality (Section 7).

- **Base unit:** a single foundational spacing unit (referred to in tokens as `space-1`) from which all other spacing derives via consistent multiples — small multiples for tight relationships (icon-to-label), larger multiples for major structural separations (between Acts/sections).
- **Margins:** page-edge margins are generous and consistent across every screen in the guest experience — Invita never lets content touch the edge of the viewport; the margin itself is part of the "fine stationery border" feeling.
- **Padding:** internal card/panel padding is always noticeably larger than the gap between unrelated adjacent elements — this asymmetry (generous internal padding, tighter external gaps) is what makes cards read as considered containers rather than arbitrary boxes.
- **Section spacing:** the transition between Act I, Act II, and Act III uses the largest spacing value in the system — these must feel like distinct chapters, never like a continuously scrolling list of equally-weighted content.
- **Card spacing:** consistent, moderate gaps between sibling cards (e.g., multiple detail cards in Act III) — enough to let each card be read as a distinct object, not so much that they feel disconnected from one another.
- **Element spacing:** the smallest spacing tier, used only between tightly related elements (an icon and its adjacent label, a value and its unit).
- **Safe Area:** all spacing scales must yield to the device safe-area inset first — no fixed margin value may ever crowd or be obscured by a notch, Dynamic Island, or home indicator; safe-area padding is additive to, never a replacement for, the standard margin.
- **Touch spacing:** interactive elements maintain a minimum surrounding clear-space buffer sufficient to prevent mis-taps on small screens, independent of the element's own visual size (an icon may look small, but its touch buffer must not be).
- **Vertical rhythm:** the guest experience is fundamentally vertical (scroll-paced storytelling) — vertical spacing values are treated as the primary rhythm-setting tool, more important than horizontal spacing, since they directly control narrative pacing (Section 24).
- **Horizontal rhythm:** kept simpler and more consistent than vertical rhythm — horizontal spacing exists mainly to establish margins and internal card padding, not to create complex multi-column compositions, which would work against the single-focus principle (Section 6).

## 14. Grid System

- **Mobile-first, single-column primary grid.** The guest experience is designed as a single vertical column of narrative content at every breakpoint from the smallest supported phone upward — Invita deliberately avoids multi-column grids in the emotional/guest-facing experience, since competing columns fight the "one focal point at a time" principle.
- **Fluid, not fixed, column width.** Content width is a percentage of viewport width within min/max bounds, so the same layout logic scales from the smallest phone to the largest, rather than jumping between discrete fixed-width layouts.
- **Safe areas govern the grid, not the reverse.** The grid's outer margin is always calculated as the larger of (a) the standard margin value or (b) the device safe-area inset — ensuring correctness on notched, Dynamic-Island, and punch-hole devices without special-casing each one.
- **Foldables:** the grid must remain single-column and legible both when a foldable is folded (narrow, tall viewport) and unfolded (wider, squarer viewport) — the unfolded state is treated as a "large phone," not as an invitation to introduce multi-column layouts, preserving the narrative pacing regardless of screen size.
- **Small phones (e.g., iPhone SE-class devices):** narrative sections may compress vertically (tighter section spacing tier) but never lose content or crop imagery — the smallest supported device sees the same story, paced slightly more tightly.
- **Large phones (e.g., Pro Max-class devices):** additional vertical breathing room is added (a larger spacing tier may apply) rather than stretching content wider — width remains bounded even on the largest phones to preserve optimal line lengths for Body text.
- **Desktop (secondary target):** the guest experience is presented within a constrained, centered, mobile-proportioned frame — never stretched edge-to-edge — reinforcing that this is fundamentally a mobile experience being respectfully viewed on a larger screen, not a responsively-reflowed desktop site.

## 15. Border Radius System

Radius in this system communicates *material softness* — corners are never sharp (which would feel cold/technical) and never excessively rounded (which would feel juvenile/playful, undermining luxury dignity). A small, consistent radius scale is used system-wide:

- **Cards / Panels:** a moderate, consistently-applied radius — soft enough to feel like rounded fine cardstock, restrained enough to still feel formal and structured.
- **Buttons:** a slightly softer/rounder radius than cards, since buttons are direct touch targets and benefit from feeling slightly more inviting/tactile.
- **Photo Frames:** minimal radius, close to a fine picture-frame corner — photography in this system is treated as art to be framed cleanly, not as a soft "app thumbnail" (see Section 18).
- **Glass Panels:** matches the card radius exactly, so glass panels and solid cards feel like members of the same family, differing only in material, not geometry.
- **Inputs:** matches button radius, reinforcing that inputs and buttons belong to the same "interactive element" visual family.
- **Dialogs / Sheets:** the largest radius in the system, applied only to the top corners when presented as a bottom sheet — signaling "this is a temporary, elevated layer above the main content."
- **Floating Objects** (e.g., a floating action element, a seal graphic housing): circular or near-circular — floating elements are the one category permitted a fully round treatment, since they are meant to read as objects (a seal, a coin, a bloom) rather than as UI containers.
- **Governing rule:** radius values step in small, consistent increments across this hierarchy (cards < buttons/inputs < dialogs < floating objects) — no two unrelated components may share a radius value coincidentally; every radius choice is a deliberate hierarchy signal.

## 16. Shadow System

Shadows in Invita simulate **candlelight and soft daylight**, never a hard technical drop-shadow. Every shadow uses a warm-tinted color (per Section 11.14), heavy blur, and low opacity.

- **Ambient Shadow:** the default, near-invisible shadow used under all elevated surfaces (cards, sheets) — its purpose is to suggest gentle lift, not to draw attention to itself. Wide blur radius, very low opacity, minimal vertical offset.
- **Glass Shadow:** slightly cooler and softer than Ambient Shadow, used specifically under glass panels to simulate the way frosted glass diffuses light around its edges rather than casting a hard silhouette.
- **Luxury Depth Shadow:** a two-layer shadow (a tight, slightly darker inner shadow plus a wide, very soft outer shadow) used for the most important elevated elements (the primary RSVP card, a modal) — this layering is what separates "flat design with a shadow" from genuine perceived depth.
- **Floating Shadow:** used under any element intended to feel like it is hovering above the surface entirely (a floating seal, a floating flower graphic in the 3D layer) — wider offset and softer blur than Ambient Shadow, to exaggerate the sense of distance from the background.
- **Ambient Occlusion-style Shadow:** subtle, tight, soft shadow used where one element sits directly against/behind another (e.g., a photo behind a gold frame) to seat objects believably rather than having them look pasted on.
- **Golden Glow:** not a shadow but a light-emission effect — a soft, warm Amber-tinted glow radiating outward from a focal object (a seal breaking open, a primary CTA button, the countdown numbers) — used sparingly, reserved for moments of genuine significance.
- **Absolute rule: shadows are never harsh.** No shadow in this system uses a small blur radius with high opacity (the combination that produces a hard, technical-looking shadow) — every shadow, without exception, is soft-edged and low-contrast against its background.

## 17. Glassmorphism Rules

Glass is used to represent **a layer floating above the "paper" of the invitation** — a modal, a navigation element, a detail card hovering over a photographic background — never as a default surface treatment for ordinary content.

- **Opacity:** glass fills sit at low-to-moderate opacity (roughly 10–25%, warm-tinted per Section 11.7) — transparent enough that the layer beneath remains recognizable, opaque enough that foreground text remains legible without an additional scrim in most cases.
- **Blur:** a moderate-to-strong background blur is applied beneath every glass surface — strong enough to abstract the background into soft color/light rather than legible shapes, since legible shapes behind text would compete with foreground content.
- **Transparency gradient:** glass panels are rarely uniformly transparent — a subtle gradient (slightly more opaque at the bottom, where text usually sits, slightly more transparent at the top, where it meets open background) improves both legibility and the sense of a physically lit material.
- **Borders:** every glass panel carries a hairline border (per Section 11.8) at slightly higher opacity than the fill itself — this thin light-catching edge is what sells the "glass" read; without it, a blurred panel simply looks like a blurred photo.
- **Reflection:** a very subtle top-edge highlight (a thin, soft light gradient along the top border) simulates a light source catching the top edge of a glass surface — used subtly, never as an obvious diagonal "glare" sheen, which reads as a dated 2010s-skeuomorphic cliché.
- **Lighting relationship:** glass panels should always visually acknowledge the Lighting System's implied light source (Section 22) — their highlight edge and shadow direction must be consistent with where "the sun" is in that particular scene.
- **Depth:** glass panels sit at a clearly higher elevation than solid cards (a stronger shadow per Section 16, a slightly larger scale on entrance) so the hierarchy of "paper" vs. "glass floating above paper" is unambiguous.
- **When to use:** navigation/utility chrome that must float above photographic or narrative content (e.g., a small floating action element during the Act II story scroll); modals and sheets that temporarily interrupt the main flow; the Act I "sealed" moment, where a glass layer over a warm glow evokes candlelit glass.
- **When NOT to use:** as the default background for large bodies of text (reduces legibility and dilutes glass's specialness); stacked more than two layers deep (multiple overlapping glass panels quickly become visually murky and lose the "clear material" quality); on top of already-busy photographic backgrounds without an additional scrim (Section 11.13) to protect text legibility.

## 18. Photo Frame System

The existing reference photograph (`invita.jpg` — a candid, sunlit garden portrait) is the canonical example against which every host-uploaded photo in the platform should be evaluated and framed.

**Frame construction (outside-in):**
1. **Outer glow** — a very soft, wide, low-opacity Golden Glow (Section 16) seated behind the frame, suggesting the photo is gently lit from within rather than sitting flatly on the page.
2. **Gold edge** — a thin Antique Gold line (Section 11.4, always gradient-built, never flat) traces the frame's outer edge — thin enough to read as a fine engraved border, never a thick, ornate "picture frame" width.
3. **Glass mat** — a very narrow band of Light/Dark Glass (matched to the surrounding surface, Section 11.7) between the gold edge and the photograph itself, simulating the fine mat board between a frame and a photograph in physical fine framing.
4. **The photograph** — presented with minimal border radius (Section 15), full color fidelity, no filter or color-grading overlay applied by the platform itself (any warmth in the image comes from the host's own photography, consistent with the brand's preference for authentic, naturally-lit imagery per Section 8 of the Foundation Document).
5. **Floating presentation** — the entire framed photograph is elevated using a Floating Shadow (Section 16), so it reads as an object placed gently above the page rather than embedded flatly within it.

**Explicit restraint:** no corner ornaments, no decorative flourishes, no drop-in filters or vignettes are added by the system. The luxury of the frame comes entirely from the quality of the gold line, the glass mat's subtlety, and the shadow's softness — "no excessive ornaments" is a hard rule, not a suggestion.

**Usage guidance:** this exact frame treatment is used consistently for every primary photograph across every future template (a couple's portrait for Wedding, a family portrait for Family Gathering, a graduate's portrait for Graduation) — the frame itself never changes; only the photograph within it changes, reinforcing system-wide consistency (Section 30).

## 19. Iconography

- **Style:** thin-stroke, geometric-but-warm line icons — never filled/solid icons (which read as heavier and more utilitarian than this brand's register) and never novelty/playful icon styles.
- **Stroke weight:** a single consistent thin stroke weight system-wide, matched proportionally to Doran Medium's stroke contrast so icons feel typographically related to the text they sit beside, rather than borrowed from an unrelated icon library.
- **Size:** icons are sized to align optically (not just numerically) with the adjacent text's cap-height, ensuring icon and label feel like a single designed unit (see 12.2's element-spacing principle).
- **Corner radius:** icon terminals use the same soft, rounded-cap treatment throughout (rounded line caps and joins) rather than sharp/mitered corners — consistent with the system's overall avoidance of harsh geometry.
- **Color:** icons default to Secondary Text color (Section 11.10) in most UI contexts; a small, deliberate subset of "ceremonial" icons (a seal, a location pin used specifically on Act III's venue card) may use the Antique Gold treatment as a highlight — most icons must not, to keep gold meaningful.
- **Animation:** icons may animate only with restrained, purposeful motion (a gentle fade/scale on appearance, a soft glow pulse for a live/active state such as a countdown) — never spin, bounce, or wiggle, which would break the brand's dignified personality.
- **Interaction:** on touch, icons receive the same gentle, immediate feedback described in Section 11 of the Foundation Document (subtle scale/glow) — never a hard color-invert or abrupt state flash.
- **Consistency:** exactly one icon style is used system-wide, sourced/drawn as a single custom set rather than mixed from multiple third-party icon libraries, to guarantee the stroke-weight and cap-style consistency above.

## 20. Illustration Style

Illustration is used sparingly, only where photography or 3D art direction (Section 21) cannot carry the moment (e.g., an abstract decorative motif, a subtle background pattern echoing a botanical or ceremonial motif per occasion).

- **Style:** fine-line, editorial illustration in the spirit of botanical field-guide plates or engraved invitation motifs — never flat vector "corporate memphis" style, never cartoon or mascot-style illustration.
- **Depth:** illustrations carry subtle depth through fine linework density and layered opacity (lighter background elements, more defined foreground elements) rather than through cell-shaded flat color blocking.
- **Lighting:** any shaded illustration follows the same implied warm, single-source lighting used throughout the system (Section 22) — light from one consistent direction, soft falloff, no flat ambient-only shading.
- **Color:** illustrations are rendered in the muted palette (Section 11) — typically Ink-toned linework with restrained Botanical Sage or Gold accents — never introducing bright, saturated colors outside the established system.
- **Texture:** a very subtle paper-grain or fine-line texture may be used to reinforce the "fine stationery" material feeling, applied uniformly and subtly, never as a heavy, distracting overlay.

# PART III — DIMENSIONAL & MOTION SYSTEMS

## 21. 3D Art Direction

All 3D elements (rendered via Three.js — art direction only, no scene/code specification here) exist to make the *letter* feel physically present and alive. Every object below must read as a genuine, premium physical object first, and a digital effect second. **Never cartoonish** is the standing rule across every object: no exaggerated proportions, no toy-like glossy plastic materials, no bright saturated cartoon coloring.

- **Envelope:** the primary Act I object. Material: fine, matte cardstock in Invita Ivory with a very subtle paper-fiber texture; edges catch a thin Foil Gold highlight. Motion: opens slowly, along a believable hinge/fold physics, never snapping open instantly. Never rendered glossy or plastic-looking.
- **Wax Seal:** matte-to-satin deep Dusk Plum or Ink-toned wax (never glossy candy-red "cartoon wax"), bearing a simple, restrained emblem (a monogram-style mark, not an illustrative scene). Motion: cracks and lifts with a soft, weighty motion — the single most "precious object" moment in the system and should be treated with the most material care.
- **Flowers:** based on the botanical, natural mood of the brand reference photography — soft, slightly desaturated petals (never neon or candy-bright), naturalistic (not perfectly symmetrical/stylized) petal forms, fine stem/leaf detail. Used as accent set-dressing (Act I/II), never as an overwhelming field of flowers.
- **Leaves:** botanical sage-toned, matte, naturalistic silhouettes (olive, eucalyptus, or similarly fine-leafed references rather than broad, cartoonish tropical leaves) — used for framing/floating accents consistent with the Housewarming template's home/garden theme, and reusable as an ambient accent across nature-adjacent future templates.
- **Gift Box:** matte cardstock or fine fabric-wrapped material finish, finished with a thin Antique Gold ribbon/cord (see Ribbon below) — proportioned as a real gift box would be (not exaggeratedly cartoon-cube-shaped), reserved for occasions where gifting is thematically central (Housewarming, Wedding, Baby Shower).
- **House:** for the Housewarming template specifically — rendered as a soft, warm, minimal architectural silhouette (evocative of modern-luxury residential architecture per the Foundation Document's "premium interior design" influence) rather than a literal, detailed dollhouse-style model; lit from one warm interior/window light source to evoke "a home, glowing, waiting to be filled."
- **Particles:** fine, soft-focus points of warm light (echoing candle-lit dust motes or golden-hour backlit particles) — small, low-opacity, slow-drifting; never large, bright, or fast-moving "confetti-style" particles, which would break the brand's calm register.
- **Ribbon:** matte fabric or thin metallic-gold cord, always rendered with soft, physically believable drape/curve (gravity-aware, never rigid or perfectly geometric) — used to tie/finish gift boxes and as a decorative accent on seals or frame corners.
- **Light Rays:** soft, low-contrast volumetric light shafts (as if sunlight is passing through a window or through garden leaves) used as atmospheric set-dressing behind Act I/II moments — always soft-edged and warm-toned, never sharp "god-ray" sci-fi beams.
- **Floating Dust:** the finest, most ambient particle layer in the system — barely-visible, softly lit motes providing a sense of a physical, sunlit room's atmosphere; always in the background, never a foreground focal element.

**Cross-cutting 3D rule:** every object above shares one coherent material and lighting world — the same warm key light (Section 22), the same muted palette (Section 11), the same soft, unhurried motion pacing (Section 24). No single 3D object may be rendered in a style, lighting scheme, or color saturation inconsistent with the others.

## 22. Lighting System

Invita's entire visual world is lit as if by **late-afternoon sun through leaves, or a single warm lamp in a quiet room** — never by flat, even, "product photography" studio lighting, and never by cold, blue-toned "tech" lighting.

- **Ambient Light:** a soft, warm, low-intensity fill light present in every scene, ensuring nothing ever falls into fully unlit black shadow — consistent with the brand's warmth-first emotional target.
- **Golden Light:** the system's signature key light — warm amber-toned, positioned to create soft, directional highlights (on gold elements, on the edges of glass, on 3D objects) — this is the single most brand-defining lighting quality in the entire system.
- **Sunset Light:** used specifically for Act I "arrival" moments and romantic-occasion templates (Wedding, Engagement) — a warmer, lower-angle variant of Golden Light, evoking golden hour specifically.
- **Soft Bloom:** a gentle light-bleed/glow applied around bright highlights (candle-like light sources, the gold seal, active countdown numbers) — simulates the way real warm light softly overexposes at its edges; always subtle, never a heavy-handed "HDR glow" effect.
- **Depth (light falloff):** foreground elements receive slightly brighter, warmer light; background elements recede into cooler, dimmer tones — this consistent falloff is what gives flat screens a sense of physical depth.
- **Reflections:** used sparingly and only on genuinely reflective materials (glass, gold, wax) — reflections should be soft and diffused (evoking ambient room reflection), never sharp mirror-like reflections, which would look artificial against the system's otherwise soft material world.
- **Natural highlights:** every lit material (gold, glass, wax, leaves) carries a single, consistent highlight direction matching the Golden Light source — inconsistent highlight direction across elements on the same screen is a direct violation of this system (see Section 29).

---

## 23. Background System

- **Luxury textures:** extremely subtle — a barely-there paper grain, a soft vignette, a faint botanical motif — always subordinate to foreground content, never competing for attention.
- **Soft gradients:** per the Gradient System (Section 11.15), used as the primary background treatment across most screens — Ivory-based gradients in daylight moments, Deep Background-based gradients in Act I/ceremonial moments.
- **Depth:** backgrounds are built in soft, blurred layers (a distant blurred layer of light/particles, a nearer, slightly sharper layer of texture) to create parallax-ready depth without introducing any hard-edged shapes.
- **Blur:** background blur increases with "distance" from the foreground narrative content — the background should never be so sharp that it visually competes with foreground text and imagery.
- **Floating particles:** the Particles and Floating Dust 3D elements (Section 21) double as a core background system element across most templates — used consistently, at low density, as connective atmosphere between narrative sections.
- **Light atmosphere:** the overall background mood across the entire platform should read, at a glance, as "a warmly lit room" or "a garden at golden hour" — never as a flat, neutral, colorless void, and never as a busy, detailed environment that distracts from the narrative content in front of it.

## 24. Motion Design System

### Animation Philosophy
Motion exists to **pace the reading of a letter**, not to demonstrate technical capability. Every animation must be traceable to one of: revealing information, directing attention, or reinforcing emotional pacing (per the Foundation Document's Animation Philosophy, Section 12). This system adds concrete timing/easing specification on top of that philosophy.

### Animation Speed & Duration
- **Micro-interactions** (icon feedback, button press response): fast enough to feel immediately responsive, but never instantaneous/robotic — a brief, soft duration that reads as "gentle acknowledgment."
- **Element reveals** (a line of text, a card entering view): moderate duration — long enough to be perceived as a deliberate reveal, short enough to never feel sluggish or block the guest's ability to keep progressing through the story.
- **Section/Act transitions** (Act I → Act II, opening the envelope): the longest, most deliberate durations in the system — these are the brand's signature cinematic moments and are allowed to take their time, within the bounds of never testing the guest's patience.

### Animation Easing
- All motion uses soft, natural deceleration curves (ease-out for elements entering, ease-in-out for elements transitioning between two states) — nothing in this system uses linear motion or sharp mechanical "ease-in-then-stop" curves, which read as robotic rather than organic.
- Signature "reveal" moments (the seal cracking, the envelope opening) use a slightly more pronounced, weighted easing curve — motion that feels like it has real mass and resistance, rather than a weightless UI slide.

### Scroll Timing
- Scroll-driven narrative sections advance content at a pace tied naturally to scroll input, but with subtle "scroll friction/damping" applied so the narrative never feels like it's simply obeying a raw scroll event — the guest should feel like they're gently turning pages, not just scrolling a webpage.
- Between major narrative beats, a brief pause/settle point may be used so content fully resolves before the next beat begins revealing — avoiding overlapping, muddled transitions.

### Hover Timing
- Hover is a secondary, desktop-only consideration (per the Mobile-First Philosophy) — where it exists (the Host dashboard, desktop preview), hover feedback is quick and subtle (a gentle brightness/glow shift), never a slow, showy hover animation that would feel out of place on a utility surface.

### Touch Timing
- Touch feedback (press states) is near-instantaneous in *initiation* (the guest must feel the tap registered immediately) but may resolve its visual settle (e.g., a glow fading back down) over a slightly longer, gentler duration — this "fast in, slow out" pattern is what makes touch feel both responsive and soft simultaneously.

### Page Transitions
- Transitions between major experience states (e.g., Guest Journey → RSVP confirmation) use a full, considered cross-fade/dissolve treatment consistent with the Reveal Overlay (Section 11.13) — never an abrupt hard cut, and never a generic "slide-in-from-the-side" pattern borrowed from standard mobile-app navigation, which would undercut the cinematic, letter-like feeling.

### Micro-interactions
- Reserved for moments that reward attention: a gentle glow when a name is revealed, a soft particle drift when a gift box is "opened," a subtle pulse on the RSVP button before first interaction to invite (not demand) action.

### Governing Rule — Never Overuse Animation
- **A hard ceiling applies:** at any given moment on screen, no more than one "signature" animated moment (a full section transition, a 3D object interaction) should be active — ambient/background motion (particles, soft bloom pulsing) is allowed to run continuously at very low visual intensity, but foreground, attention-demanding animation must never stack or compete. If in doubt, the correct choice is always to remove an animation, not add one.

## 25. Audio Design

Audio is an optional, guest-controlled enhancement layer — never forced, always instantly mutable, and never the only channel for meaningful information.

- **Background music style:** soft, warm, minimal instrumental — solo piano, gentle strings, or a similarly understated acoustic palette; tempo and mood shift subtly per occasion accent (Section 30) but always within a calm, non-intrusive register — never lyrics, never anything resembling upbeat commercial/pop production, which would clash with the brand's dignified personality.
- **Interaction sounds:** extremely subtle, soft "foley"-style sounds tied to specific meaningful gestures only (not every tap) — a soft paper rustle, a gentle chime — always short, quiet, and non-repetitive so they never become grating on replay.
- **Envelope sound:** a soft paper/card movement sound paired with the Act I envelope opening — the platform's single most important sound cue, and must be recorded/selected to sound genuinely tactile (real paper/wax), never a synthesized "UI whoosh."
- **Paper opening sound:** used consistently anywhere content is framed as "unfolding" or "revealing" a letter (Act II transitions) — same sonic family as the envelope sound, reinforcing the letter metaphor sonically as well as visually.
- **Mute experience:** the entire experience must be fully complete, coherent, and emotionally effective with audio off by default or muted — audio is a garnish, never a load-bearing part of the storytelling; a guest opening the invitation in a quiet public place must lose nothing essential by muting it.
- **Volume transitions:** all audio fades in/out smoothly (matching the soft, unhurried motion philosophy) — audio never starts or stops abruptly, and background music ducks (lowers) gracefully under any interaction sound rather than playing over it at full volume.

# PART IV — INCLUSIVE & TECHNICAL SYSTEMS

## 26. Accessibility

- **Color contrast:** every text/background color pairing in Sections 11 and 12 must be validated against WCAG AA contrast minimums in its actual usage context — the muted, warm palette's low-contrast aesthetic intent must never be used as an excuse to ship insufficient contrast; where the "ideal" muted tone fails contrast, the tone is adjusted within the same warm family until it passes, rather than accepted as-is.
- **Reduced motion:** every signature animation (Section 24) has a defined reduced-motion alternative — typically a simple, soft cross-fade replacing any larger transform/transition — so guests with motion sensitivity receive a calmer but equally complete experience, never a broken or empty one.
- **Readable typography:** minimum type sizes are enforced even at the smallest tiers (Caption, Footer) — Doran's letterforms and the RTL/Arabic-script line-height guidance (Section 12.3) are followed strictly at every size to preserve legibility, never sacrificed for a tighter visual composition.
- **Touch targets:** every interactive element meets a minimum touch-target size and surrounding touch-spacing buffer (Section 13) regardless of how visually minimal or small the element's icon/label appears.
- **Screen readers:** every visual/narrative beat (Act I/II/III) must have a coherent, meaningful non-visual reading order — a screen-reader user must be able to access host names, occasion, date, time, location, and RSVP action fully, even if they never perceive the 3D art direction, motion, or audio layers at all.

---

## 27. Responsive Rules

- **iPhone SE (smallest supported viewport):** narrative sections compress vertically (tighter Section Spacing tier per Section 13) without cropping content or imagery; Display/Hero type steps down one tier from its default size to preserve safe margins, never below the accessible minimum.
- **Modern iPhones (standard through Pro Max):** the default scale and spacing tiers apply as specified throughout this document; Pro Max-class devices receive additional vertical breathing room (Section 14) rather than wider content.
- **Android:** the same fluid, percentage-based grid (Section 14) accommodates Android's wider diversity of aspect ratios and resolutions without device-specific special-casing, provided the safe-area-driven margin rule is respected.
- **Foldables:** folded state is treated as a narrow phone; unfolded state is treated as a large phone (per Section 14) — the single-column, narrative-paced layout is preserved in both states.
- **Landscape:** the guest-facing narrative experience is designed and validated primarily for portrait orientation, consistent with how an invitation is naturally held and read; where landscape must be supported, vertical narrative sections may present with adjusted (typically reduced) section spacing to avoid excessive scroll length, but content and imagery are never cropped or hidden.
- **Portrait:** the default and primary orientation for the entire Guest Journey; all specifications throughout this document assume portrait unless otherwise noted.

## 28. Design Tokens

The following categories of design tokens should be formally specified (in a token specification format, not code) before development begins, each directly derived from the systems above:

- **Color tokens** — one token per named color in Section 11 (e.g., primary background, primary text, each gold material stop, each semantic color), plus per-occasion accent tokens that swap only the "accent" category (Section 11.3) between templates.
- **Typography tokens** — one token per row of the type scale in Section 12.1, each specifying: font family (Doran, always), weight (Bold/Medium only), size, line-height, and letter-spacing.
- **Spacing tokens** — the base unit and its full multiple scale from Section 13, covering margin, padding, section-spacing, card-spacing, and element-spacing categories separately even where values coincide, so each can evolve independently if needed.
- **Radius tokens** — one token per category in Section 15 (card, button, photo frame, glass panel, input, dialog, floating object).
- **Shadow tokens** — one token per shadow type in Section 16 (ambient, glass, luxury-depth, floating, ambient-occlusion, golden-glow), each specifying color, opacity, blur, and offset.
- **Glass tokens** — opacity, blur strength, and border-opacity values for Light Glass and Dark Glass (Section 11.7/17).
- **Motion tokens** — duration and easing-curve tokens per category in Section 24 (micro-interaction, element-reveal, section-transition), plus their reduced-motion equivalents (Section 26).
- **Breakpoint/grid tokens** — the min/max content-width bounds and safe-area-margin logic from Section 14.
- **Elevation tokens** — a small ordered scale (surface → card → glass panel → dialog → floating object) mapping each elevation level to its paired shadow and radius token, so "elevation" is governed as a single coherent concept rather than shadow and radius being set independently per component.

All tokens are organized so that a **template's entire visual variation** (Section 30) can be expressed as swapping a small number of accent-color and imagery tokens — every other token (typography, spacing, radius, shadow, motion) remains identical across every occasion template, by design.

# PART V — GOVERNANCE

## 29. Things Designers Must Never Do

**Color mistakes**
- Never introduce a fully saturated, "pure" hue anywhere in the product — every color must be muted/desaturated to belong to this system's warm, candlelit register.
- Never use a flat, single-tone gold — gold is always a gradient/material (Section 11.4); a flat gold fill reads as mustard-yellow, not luxury metal.
- Never use a cool, neutral (blue-grey) tone for glass, shadow, or background — warmth is non-negotiable across every material in this system, with the single deliberate exception of the Info semantic color (Section 11.12).

**Typography mistakes**
- Never introduce a second typeface family "just for one screen" or "just for a data table" — Doran's two weights (Bold/Medium) are the entire system; if a use case seems to need a third weight or a different face, the correct response is to solve it with size/spacing/color, not a new font.
- Never artificially widen letter-spacing on small Sorani body text — this breaks Arabic-script letter-joining and directly harms readability (Section 12.3).
- Never place two Bold-weight elements directly adjacent without a clear size or spacing break (Section 12.2) — this is the most common way hierarchy silently collapses.

**Spacing mistakes**
- Never let content touch the viewport edge or a safe-area boundary — margins are part of the brand's "fine stationery border," not an implementation detail to shrink under space pressure.
- Never make internal card padding smaller than the gap between unrelated sibling elements — this inversion destroys the "considered container" feeling described in Section 13.

**Visual/material mistakes**
- Never use a hard-edged, high-contrast drop shadow anywhere — see Section 16's absolute rule; a harsh shadow instantly reads as generic UI rather than luxury material.
- Never stack more than two glass layers on top of one another (Section 17) — this produces visual murkiness incompatible with "clear material" believability.
- Never add decorative ornaments to a photo frame beyond the specified gold edge/glass mat (Section 18) — corner flourishes, filters, or vignettes contradict the "no excessive ornaments" rule directly.
- Never render a 3D object (Section 21) with glossy, plastic, or exaggerated cartoon proportions — every object must first pass the test "would this look believable as a real, expensive physical object?"
- Never use more than one implied light-source direction within a single screen (Section 22) — inconsistent highlight/shadow direction across elements is one of the fastest ways to make an otherwise well-designed screen feel visually "off," even to viewers who can't articulate why.

**Animation mistakes**
- Never animate more than one foreground, attention-demanding element at the same time (Section 24) — simultaneous competing animations violate the single-focal-point principle at the heart of this brand.
- Never use linear or mechanically abrupt easing — all motion must feel organic and weighted, never robotic.
- Never ship a signature transition without a reduced-motion fallback (Section 26) — this is a launch-blocking omission, not later polish.
- Never use motion, sound, or color alone to celebrate — always in combination with restraint; a "celebration" moment (e.g., after RSVP) should still feel dignified, not like a confetti-cannon consumer-app moment.

**Cross-cutting/systemic mistakes**
- Never design a new template's visual identity from scratch — every future occasion (Section 30) must be built by varying accent color, imagery, and content within this exact system, never by introducing new fonts, new radius scales, new shadow logic, or new motion language.
- Never treat Kurdish Sorani UI text as a "translation layer" bolted onto a design conceived in English — layout, spacing, and alignment decisions must be made RTL-first, exactly as specified in Section 12.3.

## 30. The Visual Identity Manifesto

Every invitation created on Invita — بانگێشت نامە — regardless of occasion, must feel like it was written by the same hand.

A Housewarming invitation and a Wedding invitation will never share a story. They will always share a soul: the same warm ivory paper, the same two weights of Doran, the same gold that is never flat, the same glass that is never cold, the same shadows that never harden, the same light that always falls from one warm, golden direction, the same unhurried pace that never rushes a guest toward information before they have been given a feeling.

**What changes, template to template:**
- The words. The names. The photographs. The single occasion accent color, drawn from the shared, pre-approved family. The specific emotional key (Section 10) that colors Act II's storytelling. The specific 3D objects summoned for that occasion (a wax seal and ribbon for a Wedding; a soft architectural house form for a Housewarming; a graduation cap silhouette rendered with the same material discipline as everything else, for a Graduation).

**What never changes:**
- The typeface. The two weights. The spacing scale. The radius hierarchy. The shadow language. The glass rules. The three-act letter structure. The right-to-left, Sorani-first reading experience. The rule that gold is a material, not a color. The rule that motion serves the story and nothing else. The rule that every screen has exactly one thing the eye is meant to find first.

A senior designer joining this project in its twelfth template should be able to open this document, and without asking a single question, know exactly which gold gradient to use, exactly how many milliseconds a section transition should take, exactly how a photograph should be framed, and exactly why. That is the standard this document exists to guarantee — not creativity constrained, but craftsmanship made consistent, so that a *بانگێشت نامە* sent for a first home and a *بانگێشت نامە* sent for a wedding both arrive, unmistakably, from the same trusted hand.
